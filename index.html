CV

I am a fifth-year PhD student of linguistics at the University of Michigan, Ann Arbor advised by Dr. Savithry Namboodiripad, working with the Contact, Cognition and Change Lab and the Cognition, Convergence and Language Evolution research group.

I am a contact linguist investigating cross-modal language contact in sign languages and its attitudinal and experiential correlates using a mix of experimental and survey tools, and quantitative and qualitative analysis. My research is guided by the study of marginalised phenomena and languages, marginalisation in linguistics, and a goal of capturing cross-linguistic and individual variation. I draw on anthropology, social science, and cognitive science, bringing together Creole linguistics, sign language linguistics, psycholinguistics, and sociolinguistics.

My current research interests are in language attitudes and multimodal language contact, specifically mouthing in sign languages. My dissertation characterises experience with American Sign Language (ASL) and English in deaf and hard-of-hearing signers, captures differences in attitude to ASL while modulating the quantity of mouthing, and models how perceiver socialisation correlates with differences in attitudes to signing and signers. This is achieved using survey and experimental methods, multivariate statistical analysis, and regression modeling.

A practical implementation of my work is collaborative efforts within the University of Michigan's Linguistics department on incorporating minoritised languages in undergraduate linguistics classrooms, specifically Creoles and signed languages.

Research

My research spans multiple areas within linguistics, with a focus on underrepresented languages and phenomena.

Language Attitudes and Multimodal Contact

This line of research explores attitudes towards American Sign Language (ASL) and its contact with English, particularly in the context of bilingual deaf and hard-of-hearing communities. I investigate how mouthing (the visible movements of the mouth that accompany sign language) is perceived and how these attitudes are shaped by social factors.

Creole Linguistics

I am also deeply interested in Creole languages, particularly those spoken in the Caribbean. My work examines how these languages evolve and how they are treated in both academic and social contexts. This research contributes to broader discussions about language, identity, and power.

Sign Language Typology

I contribute to research in sign language typology, which aims to understand the diversity and complexity of sign languages across the world. This work is critical for challenging misconceptions about the homogeneity of sign languages and highlighting their rich linguistic variation.

Psycholinguistics and Sociolinguistics

My research also intersects with psycholinguistics and sociolinguistics, as I study how language is processed in the brain and how social factors influence language use and perception.
